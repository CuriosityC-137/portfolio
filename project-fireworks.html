Overview

Every year since 1999, my father and I — with the help of neighbors — have put on a professional fireworks show at Lake Keystone, Florida. What started as a backyard display has grown into a fully licensed, 4-barge pyromusical production with wireless DMX-controlled lighting, scripted sequences, and large-scale aerial effects over water.

As Lead Pyrotechnician for Lake Keystone Fireworks, I manage the full lifecycle: permitting and compliance with local fire marshals, show design and scripting, barge construction and deployment, safety coordination, team management, and the integration of custom DMX lighting systems.

The Evolution

This show has been a continuous engineering project — growing in scope, complexity, and ambition every year.

1999 — Humble Beginnings
Started with foam blocks glued and taped together and a 4×8 sheet of plywood. Simple consumer-grade fireworks launched from the water.
2001–2003 — Scaling Up
Doubled the size of the floating platform. Began learning the logistics of over-water pyrotechnics — anchoring, stability, and safe distances.
2008 — Going Professional
Became licensed pyrotechnicians. Transitioned to professional-grade aerial shells, mortar racks, and e-match firing systems. Expanded to multiple barges.
2010s — Pyromusical Integration
Began scripting shows to music with precisely timed sequences. Introduced electronic firing systems for synchronization across 4 independent floating barges.
2020s — DMX Lighting & Custom Engineering
Designed and 3D-printed custom wireless DMX-controlled LED lights for perimeter buoys and barges. Integrated lighting with pyrotechnic scripting for full pyromusical shows. See DMX Light project →

Show Day — Behind the Scenes

A typical July 4th show involves months of planning and a full day of setup on the water. Barges are loaded with mortar racks, aerial shells are sorted and fused, electronic firing modules are wired and tested, DMX lights are deployed to perimeter buoys, and safety zones are established and coordinated with local authorities.

Key Skills Demonstrated

  • Safety & Compliance: Maintaining strict adherence to NFPA 1123 standards, coordinating with fire marshals, and managing live pyrotechnic operations over water.
  • Project Management: Coordinating multi-month planning cycles, managing volunteer crews, and executing complex logistics for 4 independent floating platforms.
  • Permitting: Navigating local fire codes, ATF explosive storage requirements, and municipal permitting processes.
  • Design & Build: Engineering floating barge platforms, mortar rack systems, and custom 3D-printed DMX lighting enclosures.
  • Show Scripting: Programming timed pyromusical sequences synchronized to music across multiple firing modules.
  • DMX Lighting: Designing and deploying wireless DMX-controlled RGB lighting for perimeter buoys and barges.

What 25 Years Taught Me

  • Safety is the foundation of everything. Every engineering decision — from barge construction to firing system wiring — starts with "what's the worst that could happen?" No shortcut is worth the risk.
  • Incremental improvement compounds. The show went from foam blocks and plywood to a 4-barge DMX-integrated pyromusical — one upgrade at a time, every single year.
  • Real-world constraints are the best engineering teachers. Water, wind, voltage, wireless range, waterproofing, time pressure — these are problems you can't simulate. You solve them by doing.
  • Documentation matters. As the show grew, so did the need for checklists, wiring diagrams, firing scripts, and compliance records. Good engineering is reproducible engineering.
