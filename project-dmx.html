Project

Wireless LED Light DMX — Design & Build

How to enhance a pyromusical show beyond the pyrotechnics with the added difficulty of being a floating 4-barge show over water. Simple answer: lights.

Fusion 360 CAD Design Electronics Rapid Prototyping DMX Lighting

Inspiration

Every year since 1999, my father and I — with the help of some neighbors — have put on a professional fireworks show, with a habit of enhancing it each year. After becoming licensed pyrotechnicians, the job of enhancing the 4 floating barge shows got easier: we went bigger.

But that had safety limitations. We were capped at a certain size of "boom." So we asked ourselves: what else could we do to make the pyromusical show even better?

The idea: Add wireless DMX-controlled lighting to the floating perimeter buoys and on each barge. Seems easy, right?

The Challenges

Each perimeter floating buoy is independent of the others — relatively cheaply constructed from PVC pipe, weighted at one end, with a fishing buoy for flotation and a solar rechargeable static LED light on top.

The questions started piling up fast:

  • How do you power an LED light that needs to receive wireless DMX signals and process them into Red, Green, and Blue output?
  • How do you house this rats nest of wires and circuit boards inside a compact floating buoy?
  • The DMX receiver has a different operating voltage than the RGB LED strip — how do you handle that beyond a resistor that gets dangerously hot?
  • How do you ensure waterproofing, durability, and ease of assembly for repeated deployment on the water?

This turned out to be far more complex than originally anticipated — touching on electronics, power management, mechanical design, and manufacturing all at once.

Solution

3D design and print a custom light housing that could contain readily available circuit boards, batteries, and LED strip lighting sourced globally. The approach:

  • Test first, design second. Tested brightness, functionality, wireless range, and battery duration of all components before committing to a housing design — avoiding costly and lengthy redesigns.
  • Parametric CAD modeling in Fusion 360 for the enclosure, with proper placement of mounting holes, thread types, and cover fitting.
  • Iterative prototyping. Each 3D print could take up to 20 hours for all components. Test assembly of each revision ensured fit and function.

Design & Iterations

Testing

Several versions were tested — mostly for fit, some for function and wire routing efficiency. Plans were drawn and redrawn multiple times as I overcame my learning curve with Fusion 360. Each iteration brought the design closer to a reliable, compact, waterproof unit that could be quickly assembled and deployed on the water.

What I Learned

  • Test components individually before designing the housing. This saved multiple print cycles and weeks of iteration time.
  • Fusion 360 learning curve is steep but worth it. Each redesign taught new parametric modeling techniques that I now apply to every project.
  • Voltage mismatch between components required creative power management solutions beyond simple resistors.
  • Real-world constraints (waterproofing, heat dissipation, battery life, wireless range) push designs in directions you can't anticipate on screen alone.
